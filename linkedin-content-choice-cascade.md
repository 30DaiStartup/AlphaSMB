### Post 1 of 5: The Setup — "Most Leaders Put AI in the Wrong Box"

**Type:** Value / Insight (Monday post)
**Hashtags:** `#AIStrategy` `#Leadership` `#SmallBusiness` `#PlayingToWin`

```
Most leaders put AI in the wrong strategic box.

They treat it as a "how to win" question. Or worse -- a tool selection question 
buried somewhere in operations.

If you've read Roger Martin's Playing to Win, you know the choice cascade: 
winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems.

AI sits in the capabilities box. And that matters more than most people realize.

Because capabilities don't just support your current strategy. They expand what 
strategies become possible. A new capability changes where you can compete, how 
you can win, and ultimately what your business can aspire to become.

When you put AI in the wrong box, you get the wrong outcomes. You buy tools 
instead of building capability. You optimize current workflows instead of 
reimagining your competitive position.

The first step isn't picking an AI tool. It's understanding what the AI 
capability actually makes possible for your business.

That's a fundamentally different conversation.

I'm going to spend the next few posts walking through each level of the cascade 
and showing how AI capability rewrites it. If you run an SMB, this matters.
```

### Post 2 of 5: Capabilities — "You Can't Plan What You Haven't Experienced"

**Type:** Story / Pattern (Wednesday post)
**Hashtags:** `#AI` `#AITransformation` `#OrganizationalChange` `#FractionalLeadership`

```
You can't strategy-deck your way into an AI-capable organization.

This is the part that trips up most leadership teams.

In Roger Martin's choice cascade, capabilities sit near the bottom. They're the 
foundation. And with AI, there's a catch: you have to build the capability before 
you can see what it makes possible.

I see this constantly. A CEO asks me: "What's our AI strategy?" And my honest 
answer is: "You can't know yet."

Not because they're not smart enough. Because the strategic possibilities only 
become visible after your organization has experienced the capability firsthand. 
After your leadership team has shifted their mindset. After your people have 
actually used these tools on real work.

Then the lightbulbs start going off. "Wait -- if we can do this, then we 
could..." That's the moment. And it can't be manufactured in a planning session.

The order of operations matters. Capability first. Then strategy.

Most companies try to do it backwards. They plan the AI strategy, then try to 
build the capability to execute it. It doesn't work. Because they're planning 
based on what they can imagine today -- not what becomes possible once the 
capability is real.

Build the capability. Experience the shift. Then climb back up the cascade.
```

### Post 3 of 5: Where to Play — "AI Opens Doors You Didn't Know Existed"

**Type:** Value / Insight (Monday post)
**Hashtags:** `#AIStrategy` `#SmallBusiness` `#AIforSMBs` `#Leadership`

```
AI doesn't just make you better at what you already do. It lets you compete in 
places you couldn't before.

In the Playing to Win cascade, "where to play" means markets, customers, 
segments, geographies, channels. The boundaries of your competitive arena.

Most SMB leaders think about AI as a way to do current work faster. That's 
operational improvement. It's real, but it's the smallest box AI fits in.

The bigger shift: AI capability can change your where-to-play entirely.

A 50-person professional services firm that builds AI capability doesn't just 
serve clients faster. They can serve client segments that were previously 
uneconomical. They can enter adjacent markets that required expertise they 
couldn't afford to hire. They can compete with firms three times their size 
because the capability gap narrows.

A manufacturer with real AI capability doesn't just optimize production. They can 
offer customization at scale, enter direct-to-consumer channels, or provide 
data-driven services alongside physical products.

None of these show up in a "which AI tools should we buy" conversation. They only 
become visible when leadership has experienced the capability and started asking: 
"What does this make possible that wasn't possible before?"

That's a where-to-play conversation. And most SMBs haven't had it yet.

### Post 4 of 5: How to Win — "Same Market, Different Weapon"

**Type:** Story / Pattern (Wednesday post)
**Hashtags:** `#AI` `#AIStrategy` `#OrganizationalChange` `#SmallBusiness`

```
Even if you don't change where you compete, AI changes how you win there.

In the choice cascade, "how to win" is your competitive advantage in your chosen 
arena. Your edge. The reason customers pick you over the alternative.

For most SMBs, that edge has been some combination of relationships, expertise, 
speed, or price. AI doesn't replace those -- but it amplifies them in ways that 
weren't possible two years ago.

I talked to a healthcare services company last month. Same market. Same 
customers. Same team. But once they built real AI capability -- not just bought 
licenses, actually built the organizational muscle -- their how-to-win shifted.

They went from competing on responsiveness to competing on insight. Their team 
could synthesize patient data, research, and operational patterns faster than 
competitors with three times the headcount. The capability didn't change their 
market. It changed their weapon.

Here's the part that matters for SMB leaders: your larger competitors are building 
this capability right now. If they change their how-to-win and you don't, you're 
not standing still. You're falling behind.

The gap compounds. Every month you wait is a month they pull further ahead.

And the fix isn't buying the tools they're buying. They have the same tools you 
do. The fix is building the organizational capability to actually use them.

That's the difference between AI-equipped and AI-capable.
```

### Standalone 2: "The Difference Between AI-Equipped and AI-Capable"

**Type:** The Difference Between X and Y (Template 4)
**Hashtags:** `#AI` `#AITransformation` `#AIforSMBs` `#Leadership`

```
There's a difference between an AI-equipped organization and an AI-capable one.

AI-equipped looks like:
- Everyone has a ChatGPT license
- A few people use it for drafting emails
- Leadership mentions AI in quarterly updates
- Tool adoption plateaus after 30 days

AI-capable looks like:
- Leadership thinks differently about what the business can become
- There's a champion driving adoption from within
- High-agency people have permission and methods to lead
- The organization measures progress weekly against benchmarks
- AI capability is expanding where you compete and how you win

Most companies default to AI-equipped because it's easier. Buy licenses. Send a 
training email. Check the box.

But AI-equipped is why 80% of employees stop using AI tools within a month. It's 
a tool rollout, not a transformation.

AI-capable is what changes your competitive position. It's what lets a 50-person 
company compete with one ten times its size. It's what turns AI from an expense 
into a strategic capability.

The difference isn't the tools. It's the organization behind them.

One is a software purchase. The other is a strategic capability that rewrites what 
your business can become.

Which one are you building?
